- making diagnosis is just pointing at a location on a map. the real work is actually going to the territory and navigate it with the patient hand by hand. and maps can be wrong too.
- organisms as complex systems, not machines: and in complex systems, the features are emergence, and what matters for emergence is the relationships between its parts rather than the mechanics of the parts themselves as in machineries.
- pitfall of biopsychosocial model is when we still think of biological, psychological and sociological aspects in linear, machine-like, one-way causality terms. the point is not so much incorporating psychological and sociological models (they are, at its best, models after all, on top of which flesh and blood still needs to be put on), but on the aiming towards less and less abstraction and more touch on the complex reality. and as always, complexity emergence is not emergence if we can predict it. biopsychosocial is just the starting point, some better but still rough guide of where to put our finger on the map. the work is still out there in the territorial plane untouched by language: the work of human connection and mutual self-discovery.
- but physicians themselves are human beings—there are patients they can connect with and those they cannot. i think we should not force physicians to be white liars of personality and instead encourage diversity in physician's personalities e.g. referral to another family physician who's personality might be more suitable for the patient.
